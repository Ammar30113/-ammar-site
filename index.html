01 — About

I don't think of myself as support, or ops, or eng. I think of myself as the person who turns chaos into a system.

I've spent the last three years embedded inside ResQ — a fast-moving SaaS in the repair and maintenance space — coordinating between fifty-plus clients, twenty-plus vendors, and a product still finding its shape. The work is half customer obsession, half quiet engineering: noticing the same exception happen three times, then designing it out of existence.

Somewhere along the way it stopped feeling like support. I was writing AI-assisted templates, sketching Retool dashboards over weekends, and shipping bulk-data workflows that quietly replaced hours of human reconciliation. The mental model that stuck: operations is product, just for an internal audience.

The best operators I know don't fix tickets — they fix the conditions that produce them.

Now I build platforms in the same shape: QuoteStack for procurement, Rallio as local word-of-mouth infrastructure, ContentOS as a personal AI workflow stack I run daily, and ScoutBot-301 as a market-signal and decision-routing engine. The connective tissue is the same — structured workflows, AI in the right places, humans in the rest.

03 · The claim momentOwners arrive to find their profile already populated. The pitch is proof, not promise.
The problem
Local discovery has been captured by ad auctions and review-farming. Meanwhile the actual mechanism that moves people — a friend telling a friend — leaves no trail and earns no credit.
The repositioning
Treat word-of-mouth as infrastructure: every recommendation generates a receipt, every click and visit ties back to a supporter, and the city's quiet curators finally have a public ledger.
Architecture
Community-built business profiles · supporter identity & status · neighborhood taste maps · click and visit attribution · recommendation receipts. Not a feed — a system.
Positioning
Anti-paid-ad. Anti-influencer marketplace. Built on the premise that the next generation of local discovery is owned by neighborhoods, not advertisers.
PROJECT · 003

ScoutBot-301

Solo build · market-signal & decision-routing system
Systems work

A market-data orchestration and decision-routing engine designed around structured execution, failover reliability, and signal filtering. Ingests heterogeneous feeds, normalizes them into a single decision surface, and routes only the signals that pass configurable rule and reliability gates. The point isn't speed — it's defensible decisions under operational pressure.

FEEDS
primary · ok secondary · ok tertiary · degraded → failover
FILTER

ROUTE
14:02:11signal NQ-OPEN-08 · 3 sources confirmrouted → action queue

14:03:08tertiary feed timeout · auto-failover engagedsafe state
The problem
Most signal engines optimize for capture and leave reliability as an afterthought. The hard part isn't ingest — it's deciding which signals deserve to leave the system.
The bet
A decision-routing layer with explicit failover, cross-source confirmation, and configurable suppression turns noisy market data into something an operator can stake a decision on.
Architecture
Structured execution graph · sentiment and rule-based filtering · multi-feed orchestration · failover and cooldown logic · operational safeguards as first-class primitives.
Why it matters
Same operator's instinct that drives QuoteStack, Rallio, and ContentOS — make the system legible, make the failure modes obvious, make the human in the loop sharper, not redundant.

Daily driver

The system I run my own thinking through. Research capture, idea management, content drafting, and workflow organization — all stitched together with reusable prompt frameworks and modular workflows. Less a productivity app, more an internal laboratory for testing what AI-assisted operations should actually feel like.

The problem
Most "AI productivity" tools optimize for one step — capture, or summarize, or draft. The friction lives in the handoffs between them, where context evaporates and voice drifts.
The bet
If the prompts, the taxonomy, and the workflows are version-controlled artifacts — not chat-window improvisations — the same operator can think two or three times faster without sounding two or three times more generic.
Architecture
Capture layer · reusable prompt frameworks · modular workflow chains · voice constraints as configurable rules · cross-linking across research, drafts, and projects.
Why it exists
A live testbed for AI-assisted operations. Everything I'd want to ship into a team tool, I run on myself first — so by the time it leaves my own workflow, the rough edges are gone.

05 — Product thinking

A short list of things I keep coming back to. Less a manifesto, more a working set of priors.

№ 01

Operations is product, just for internal users.

Every spreadsheet, Slack handoff, and copy-paste loop is a feature waiting to be built. The teams that internalize this ship twice — once for customers, once for themselves.

№ 02

Templates beat ticketing, until they don't.

I'm bullish on AI-assisted templates and bearish on auto-reply. The right system writes the boring 80% and forces a human into the part that requires judgment. Anything else is just faster mediocrity.

№ 03

The best operators delete more than they add.

Most internal pain isn't missing tools — it's tools nobody owns. The highest-leverage move is usually a deletion: a workflow retired, a Slack channel closed, a process collapsed into a single screen.

№ 04

Voice is a constraint, not a style.

The interesting question with LLMs in writing isn't "can it sound like me?" — it's "what's the smallest set of rules that keeps me sounding like me?" Style guides as code. Voice as version-controlled artifact.

№ 05

Build the dashboard you wish existed.

Retool, Linear, Supabase — the modern operator's toolkit means you don't need to wait for engineering to fix the thing slowing you down. If the friction outlasts a sprint, the answer is usually a weekend.

№ 06

Stay close to the customer.

Three years of frontline support is the cheapest product education money can buy. I do not plan to ever fully step away from the inbox — even if the title changes.
